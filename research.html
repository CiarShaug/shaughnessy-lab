ResearchOverview

The Shaughnessy Lab investigates the physiological mechanisms that support environmental adaptation, endocrine regulation, and epithelial transport across diverse vertebrate systems. Our work integrates molecular, cellular, and whole-organism approaches, with a particular emphasis on ion transport, hormonal signaling, and stress physiology. Research in the lab spans both fundamental and applied questions, and is organized around two complementary themes: Comparative Physiology, which examines the evolution and regulation of physiological systems across taxa, and Translational Physiology, which focuses on epithelial transport and CFTR function in the context of human disease.

ComparativePhysiology

Our research in this theme is centered on environmental physiological processes and the endocrine programs that regulate them. We integrate in vivo, ex vivo, and in vitro approaches and focus our attention primarily to processes of ionoregulation, stress, and thermal tolerance. Although the majority of our studies have centered on fishes, past and ongoing work has also incuded studies on crustaceans and amphibians. Given that we work heavily on more basal linneages of fishes (i.e., hagfishes, lampreys, elasmobranchs, and non-teleosts), a major emphasis of this work that has emerged is in understanding how complexity in physiological processes evolved throughout the radiation of vertebrates, including how endocrine signaling systems or epithelial transport processes gained or lost function through genome evolution. This research is deeply integrated in a broader community of comparative physiology and endocrinology, and we regularly present our work at meetings such as the International Congress on the Biology of Fish (ICBF), the North American Society for Comparative Endocrinology (NASCE), as well as other less regular meetings.

Comparative | Epithelial Ion Transport and Osmoregulation

Our research explores how fishes maintain salt and water balance in dynamic aquatic environments. We focus on the molecular identity and regulation of ionocytes — specialized epithelial cells that mediate ion exchange — and how their function is shaped by endocrine signals and environmental change.

  • Ionocyte plasticity in freshwater and seawater fishes
  • CFTR, NKCC, and Ano1 expression and localization
  • Regulation by corticosteroids, prolactin, and vasotocin

Neuroendocrine Control of Stress and Adaptation

We examine how endocrine systems respond to physiological stress in early vertebrates. Our studies focus on the hypothalamus–pituitary–interrenal (HPI) axis, exploring hormonal signaling pathways and receptor function that support adaptation to osmotic, thermal, and metabolic challenges.

  • Functional characterization of melanocortin signaling systems
  • Cortisol and glucose regulation in basal fishes
  • Environmental regulation of the HPI axis in lamprey and sturgeon

Evolution of Hormone Signaling Systems

We explore the structural and functional evolution of endocrine signaling pathways in early vertebrates, with an emphasis on melanocortin and CRH receptor families. Using molecular, pharmacological, and phylogenetic approaches, we investigate how receptor diversification supports endocrine control of stress and metabolism.

  • Comparative endocrinology across jawless and jawed fishes
  • Evolution of MRAP accessory proteins and GPCR trafficking
  • Ancestral sequence reconstruction of hormone-receptor systems

Applied Environmental Physiology

Our applied work investigates physiological responses of native and non-native fishes to changing environmental conditions. We collaborate with state agencies and conservation partners to inform fisheries management, with a focus on thermal tolerance and ecological physiology in vulnerable species.

  • Thermal tolerance comparisons in Neosho and Smallmouth Bass hybrids
  • Species distribution modeling based on physiological limits
  • Undergraduate-led research on osmoregulation and environmental stress

TranslationalPhysiology

Our translational work focuses on epithelial transport and CFTR regulation in the context of cystic fibrosis. This research leverages in vitro models and electrophysiological techniques to investigate the molecular mechanisms of CFTR function and pharmacological modulation. While our lab does not directly aim to develop therapies, our goal is to contribute to the foundational understanding of epithelial signaling and CFTR function in ways that may inform therapeutic innovation. This work connects us with the broader cystic fibrosis research community, particularly through collaborations and scientific exchange supported by the Cystic Fibrosis Foundation (CFF) and the Oklahoma Center for Respiratory and Infectious Diseases (OCRID). We also engage with research presented at the North American Cystic Fibrosis Conference (NACFC) and related professional meetings.

CFTR Function and Regulation

We study the molecular mechanisms governing CFTR channel function in human airway epithelia. This work focuses on the regulation of CFTR by receptor-mediated pathways and explores how epithelial context influences CFTR conductance, trafficking, and activation in both normal and disease models.

  • Signal transduction mechanisms that activate CFTR via GPCRs
  • Electrophysiological and biochemical analyses of CFTR channel function
  • Functional comparisons of airway epithelial cell types

CF Therapeutics and Disease Modeling

Our applied studies in cystic fibrosis use airway epithelial models to assess pharmacological responses to CFTR modulators. We evaluate drug efficacy and synergy in vitro using electrophysiological tools, and work toward improving screening approaches for difficult-to-treat mutations.

  • Functional testing of CFTR modulators including ivacaftor, elexacaftor, and lubiprostone
  • Ussing chamber and voltage clamp studies in primary and immortalized epithelia
  • Development of predictive assays for therapeutic responses in CF models
